Is robotics repeating the AR/VR hype cycle?

LS With Lex Sokolin ↗, Managing Partner and Co-Founder of Generative Ventures

Lex invests at Generative Ventures, at the intersection of fintech, AI, and crypto, a thesis he calls the machine economy. He argues robotics is a retread of the LLM race with far more friction, warns that the hype will outrun physical reality the way AR/VR did in 2018, and makes the case that durable value sits in infrastructure, data access, and the system around the model, not the model itself.

These are the written field notes from the conversation.

Lex is an investor, not a roboticist, and that is the point. His read is a caution: robotics may be running the same script augmented reality ran in 2018, and where the money ends up is a separate question from whether the technology is ready.

Robotics looks a lot like AR/VR in 2018

Back then, augmented and virtual reality had the exact shape robotics has now: a hardware-shipment story, big-name players, and an “every iPhone becomes a headset” narrative. It topped out around ten million headsets a year and became, in his words, a nothing burger. Today only five to seven thousand Unitree robots shipped, while Figure carries a roughly forty-billion-dollar valuation with no real revenue. Narrative volume vastly exceeds deployment volume. And the sharp caveat: being smart and doing the right trade are separate things. You can be right that it is too early and still be wrong about where the capital goes.

Where value actually accrues

If robotics is a retread of the language-model race, the lesson from that race is about where value sticks. There is no long-term defensibility in having the best model, because open source catches up and compresses it fast. Defensibility moved to the system around the model: the harness, the integrations, the data, evaluation, and deployment. In the boom, infrastructure, memory, and compute paid off, while selling AI products did not. The model is a depreciating asset.

The same moat, confirmed three times

The most useful thread runs through all three episodes. Data companies that bootstrap datasets to sell to labs are undifferentiated unless they have privileged access. The one Lex backed cleared the bar only because it had access to two factories. Data access is a relationship and commercial moat, not a technical one, the same conclusion Manako and BitRobot reached from the operator side.

Follow the capital

Put it together and the capital chain is legible. Big-tech balance sheets, redirected from buybacks into the AI race, flow into infrastructure, then into the labs, then toward applications that have yet to earn. Infrastructure is the reliable place to stand, and robotics is running the same play, with more friction.
