Why vision is still waiting for its GPT moment

MS With Max Sebti ↗, CEO of Manako Labs

Manako builds vision agents that run entirely on-device, in 19MB models that turn existing camera networks into real-time understanding. Max argues computer vision has no consensus training recipe yet, unpacks why that gap defines the field, and makes the case that the data layer, not the learning paradigm, is the durable place to stand while everything is still unsettled.

“Vision is solved” is a category error.
Generative image quality and VLM captioning aren't physical understanding. Say vision is solved and you erase robotics from the picture, because robotics runs on vision.
There is no playbook. More than ten live approaches.
Imitation learning, RL, sim-to-real, VLAs, diffusion policies, learning from video. No consensus, because nobody has cracked zero-shot competence in a situation the robot has never seen.
Data doesn't just train the model. It dictates the architecture.
You can't stream video 24/7 to the cloud; there aren't enough data centers. So models go tiny and on-device, and curation moves into the model itself.
“Being intelligent isn't what you know. It's what you do when you don't know.”
Max's working definition of a world model: intelligence that adapts without being trained for the situation in front of it.

Full episode notes
The state of the field
  • Vision is still waiting for its “GPT moment.” Transformers unlocked language. Nothing equivalent has landed for vision, so there's no consensus training recipe.
  • The “vision is solved” claim is a category error. It conflates generative image quality and captioning with physical understanding, and it implicitly erases robotics.
  • No playbook exists. More than ten live approaches are in play (imitation learning, RL, sim-to-real, offline RL, learning from video, VLAs, model-based RL, self-supervised exploration, diffusion policies), because nobody has cracked zero-shot competence in an unseen situation.
The two camps on world models
  • Predictive (LeCun and JEPA): don't rely on seeing. Predict embeddings or latent states. Max leans this way.
  • Generative: the best way to predict what's next is to generate the future as you move.
  • NVIDIA straddles both. It sells GPUs either way.
  • Working definition of a world model: intelligence that adapts without being trained for the situation.
Data is the bottleneck, and it dictates architecture
  • Most training techniques bottom out in collecting visual data. That is why capital is flooding into training facilities and camera-headset recording rigs.
  • You physically can't stream video 24/7 to the cloud, so models must run on-device: tiny (~19MB, CPU-runnable) and low-energy.
  • Edge models filter at the source, capturing true positives only. Data curation becomes part of the model, not a post-processing step.
  • Expect a data wall, and labs starting to share datasets as scarcity bites.
A training approach for small teams
  • Distillation is the on-ramp. The “Qwen playbook” is to start from a large teacher (~72B) and distill down. Cheaper and faster than building from scratch.
  • Reasoning matters more than assumed: start from a reasoning model and teach it to see, rather than bolting reasoning onto a vision model.
  • Their VLA is a deliberate “Frankenstein”: a distilled reasoning model, a traditional VLM, and a pure detection model, running roughly ten bespoke primitives.
  • Their most advanced VLM was distilled from NVIDIA Cosmos (the reasoning model). Synthetic and sim data is still on the exploration list.
Real-world constraints researchers underrate
  • Low-res is fine. Benchmarks run at 640 to 1088px, so 15-year-old cameras still work at the pixel level. Don't let sensor quality gate data collection. Start now.
  • The hard cases are the point: occlusion, rain, snow, garbage footage. Football was the proving ground (22 agents in continuous chaos), and structure that survives that generalises to stores and stations.
  • Data access is a commercial problem, not a technical one. Enterprises won't hand over sensors until you solve a real problem first. The flywheel is to deploy, gain camera access, then keep updating weights from real operations.
  • Enterprise self-serve is economically impossible. On-site reasoning plus vision needs A100s (H100s for real time) across tens of thousands of sites. That is the structural case for tiny on-device models.
The strategic read for researchers
  • Max is explicit that he is “upstream.” He is deliberately on the sidelines of the methodology race, collecting data and building models that reason over it while waiting to see which camp cracks it.
  • The takeaway: the data layer is the durable position while the learning paradigm is still unsettled.
