Episode 02 · Preview

Is robot learning a data problem, or an architecture problem?

NP MC With Nils Pihl ↗ — Founder & CEO, Auki Labs · and Michael Cho ↗ — Co-founder, BitRobot (FrodoBots Lab)

BitRobot's Michael Cho is building global infrastructure to collect in-the-wild robot data at scale; Auki's Nils Pihl thinks the data-collection gold rush is a mistake. Two credible operators with real deployments disagree on the central question — is embodied intelligence bottlenecked by data or by architecture — and the disagreement is the signal.

Evaluation is the real bottleneck — you can't speed up reality.
Digital models iterate fast because benchmarks are near-instant. Physical AI is the opposite: a million zero-disengagement FSD miles still wouldn't prove L4 vs L5, because the next mile might crash. Edge cases get rarer at the tail, so confidence gets harder to earn the closer you get.
The subtask math is brutal: 0.99¹⁰⁰ ≈ 30%.
Frying an egg is ~100 sequential subtasks. Even at a generous 99% each, end-to-end success collapses to ~30–37%. The frontier is long-horizon reliability and recovery — roughly six nines per subtask — not marginal per-task gains.
Mosquito intelligence: 0.1W, no pretraining.
A mosquito navigates the physical world data-efficiently on a fraction of a watt, never pretrained on millions of hours. Biology is proof a radically more efficient approach exists — a real argument that scaling is not the answer here.
Data problem, or architecture problem?
Michael bets on in-the-wild data at massive scale and is building for it. Nils calls the data-collection boom “one of the dumbest moments of VC groupthink ever.” The unresolved tension is arguably the open question in robot learning.
Full episode notes
The two strongest technical insights
  • Evaluation is the rate limiter, and it can't be compressed. Digital models iterate fast because benchmark results are near-instant. Physical AI is the opposite — even a million zero-disengagement FSD miles wouldn't tell you L4 vs L5, because the next mile might crash. Edge cases get rarer as you approach the tail, so confidence gets harder the closer you are. The bottleneck is eval infrastructure, not model capacity.
  • The subtask compounding math is a usability problem, not a safety one. SOTA short-horizon success sits ~70–80%. Frying an egg ≈ 100 sequential subtasks; even at 99% each, 0.99^100 ≈ 30–37% end-to-end. You need ~six nines per subtask plus strong recovery. Long-horizon reliability and error recovery deserve far more attention than marginal per-task gains.
Where the field's assumptions may be wrong
  • “Mosquito intelligence.” ~0.1W, extraordinarily data-efficient, never pretrained on millions of hours of flight. Biology proves a fundamentally different, radically more efficient approach to physical intelligence exists — a real argument that scaling is not the answer.
  • Language pretraining doesn't transfer to perception and movement. The entire internet text corpus isn't a meaningful leap toward teaching a robot to see and move. VLAs translate intent (“unscrew the bottle cap”) but that doesn't mean the model knows what the hands must do — that data doesn't exist yet.
  • A contrarian shot at the data-collection boom. Nils is openly bearish on VC-funded robot data collection, predicting we'll look back on it as “one of the dumbest moments of VC groupthink ever.” If the mosquito point holds, the bottleneck is architecture, not more teleop hours.
  • Embodied models are compressions of their datasets. Train on a handful of Airbnb kitchens and you overfit to them — every kitchen is similar and every kitchen is unique. The honest case for in-the-wild data, in direct tension with the point above. The episode doesn't resolve it, which is itself the signal.
Epistemic hygiene for reading the field
  • Treat a demo as an upper bound, not a capability. Assume the one-minute video is the ceiling and may have taken hours of takes. Uncut, hours-long runs are the only real proof — many impressive humanoid demos are still substantially teleoperated.
  • Where we actually are: Michael puts robotics at roughly the GPT-2 moment — qualitatively new versus a year ago, but a large gap before the GPT-3-style wake-up. His timeline: digital AGI within a few years, robotics still unsolved. Physical AI is where the remaining hard research lives.
Practical resources researchers can use
  • BitRobot's explicit model: do the work “researchers wanted done but shouldn't have spent time doing” — data collection, compute aggregation, and real-world evaluation of models on physical robots distributed globally.
  • Concrete proof: a UC Berkeley lab built a SOTA urban-navigation model on the FrodoBots dataset and evaluated it on robots in Africa and South America — geographic diversity no single lab can obtain. Human-vs-AI competitions drew people from Google DeepMind, Berkeley, NUS, and UT Austin; they're releasing what they claim is the largest urban-navigation dataset of its kind on Hugging Face.
  • Data IP default: open-source for non-commercial use, with commercial licensing split between the subnet owner and the foundation — worth knowing if you're evaluating data provenance.
  • The cheap-hardware insight: a $10,000 robot is a bad data-collection device. They built a ~$500 teleoperated “dumb” robot and made data collection a byproduct of a game people wanted to play.
Two constraints researchers systematically underweight
  • Privacy will shape what data is legally and socially available. Both 1X and Tesla have said openly they intend to build the largest training set in the world by recording inside people's homes. Expect friction — an argument for on-device, federated, and self-hosted architectures over centralized hoarding (Auki deliberately collects no data).
  • The environment is a design variable, not a fixed constraint. Cities were reshaped for cars; they'll be reshaped for robots. If SLAM and visual positioning fail on repeating patterns, buildings will adopt non-repeating “visual entropy” surfaces — minimalism and repeating tiles disappear. Plus battery-swap stations, repair shops, drone pads. You don't always have to solve perception in a hostile environment when co-designing the environment is on the table — which also punctures the “humanoids are perfectly suited to our world” story.
The open question
  • The two guests disagree productively: Michael thinks in-the-wild data at massive scale is the unlock and is building infrastructure for it; Nils thinks the data-collection gold rush is a mistake. Both are credible operators with real deployments.
  • Is embodied intelligence a data problem or an architecture problem? That unresolved tension is arguably the open question in robot learning right now.

Episode 01 · Preview

Why vision is still waiting for its GPT moment

MS With Max Sebti ↗ — CEO, Manako Labs

Manako builds vision agents that run entirely on-device — 19MB models that turn existing camera networks into real-time understanding. Max argues computer vision has no consensus training recipe yet, unpacks why that gap defines the field, and makes the case that the data layer — not the learning paradigm — is the durable place to stand while everything is still unsettled.

“Vision is solved” is a category error.
Generative image quality and VLM captioning aren't physical understanding. Say vision is solved and you erase robotics from the picture — because robotics runs on vision.
There is no playbook — 10+ live approaches.
Imitation learning, RL, sim-to-real, VLAs, diffusion policies, learning from video. No consensus, because nobody has cracked zero-shot competence in a situation the robot has never seen.
Data doesn't just train the model — it dictates the architecture.
You can't stream video 24/7 to the cloud; there aren't enough data centers. So models go tiny and on-device, and curation moves into the model itself.
“Being intelligent isn't what you know — it's what you do when you don't know.”
Max's working definition of a world model: intelligence that adapts without being trained for the situation in front of it.
Full episode notes
The state of the field
  • Vision is still waiting for its “GPT moment.” Transformers unlocked language; nothing equivalent has landed for vision, so there's no consensus training recipe.
  • The “vision is solved” claim is a category error — it conflates generative image quality and captioning with physical understanding, and implicitly erases robotics.
  • No playbook exists. 10+ live approaches (imitation learning, RL, sim-to-real, offline RL, learning from video, VLAs, model-based RL, self-supervised exploration, diffusion policies) — because nobody has cracked zero-shot competence in an unseen situation.
The two camps on world models
  • Predictive (LeCun / JEPA): don't rely on seeing — predict embeddings or latent states. Max leans this way.
  • Generative: the best way to predict what's next is to generate the future as you move.
  • NVIDIA straddles both — it sells GPUs either way.
  • Working definition of a world model: intelligence that adapts without being trained for the situation.
Data is the bottleneck — and it dictates architecture
  • Most training techniques bottom out in collecting visual data — hence the capital flooding into training facilities and camera-headset recording rigs.
  • You physically can't stream video 24/7 to the cloud, so models must run on-device: tiny (~19MB, CPU-runnable), low-energy.
  • Edge models filter at the source — capturing true positives only. Data curation becomes part of the model, not a post-processing step.
  • Expect a data wall — and labs starting to share datasets as scarcity bites.
A training approach for small teams
  • Distillation is the on-ramp — the “Qwen playbook”: start from a large teacher (~72B) and distill down. Cheaper and faster than building from scratch.
  • Reasoning matters more than assumed: start from a reasoning model and teach it to see, rather than bolting reasoning onto a vision model.
  • Their VLA is a deliberate “Frankenstein”: a distilled reasoning model + a traditional VLM + a pure detection model, running ~10 bespoke primitives.
  • Their most advanced VLM was distilled from NVIDIA Cosmos (the reasoning model). Synthetic/sim data is still on the exploration list.
Real-world constraints researchers underrate
  • Low-res is fine. Benchmarks run at 640–1088px, so 15-year-old cameras still work at the pixel level. Don't let sensor quality gate data collection — start now.
  • The hard cases are the point: occlusion, rain, snow, garbage footage. Football was the proving ground — 22 agents in continuous chaos — and structure that survives that generalises to stores and stations.
  • Data access is a commercial problem, not a technical one. Enterprises won't hand over sensors until you solve a real problem first. The flywheel: deploy → gain camera access → keep updating weights from real operations.
  • Enterprise self-serve is economically impossible — on-site reasoning + vision needs A100s (H100s for real time) across tens of thousands of sites. That's the structural case for tiny on-device models.
The strategic read for researchers
  • Max is explicit that he's “upstream” — deliberately on the sidelines of the methodology race, collecting data and building models that reason over it while waiting to see which camp cracks it.
  • The takeaway: the data layer is the durable position while the learning paradigm is still unsettled.
