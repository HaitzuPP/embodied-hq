Episode 01 · Preview

Why vision is still waiting for its GPT moment

MS With Max Sebti ↗ — CEO, Manako Labs

Manako builds vision agents that run entirely on-device — 19MB models that turn existing camera networks into real-time understanding. Max argues computer vision has no consensus training recipe yet, unpacks why that gap defines the field, and makes the case that the data layer — not the learning paradigm — is the durable place to stand while everything is still unsettled.

“Vision is solved” is a category error.
Generative image quality and VLM captioning aren't physical understanding. Say vision is solved and you erase robotics from the picture — because robotics runs on vision.
There is no playbook — 10+ live approaches.
Imitation learning, RL, sim-to-real, VLAs, diffusion policies, learning from video. No consensus, because nobody has cracked zero-shot competence in a situation the robot has never seen.
Data doesn't just train the model — it dictates the architecture.
You can't stream video 24/7 to the cloud; there aren't enough data centers. So models go tiny and on-device, and curation moves into the model itself.
“Being intelligent isn't what you know — it's what you do when you don't know.”
Max's working definition of a world model: intelligence that adapts without being trained for the situation in front of it.
Full episode notes
The state of the field
  • Vision is still waiting for its “GPT moment.” Transformers unlocked language; nothing equivalent has landed for vision, so there's no consensus training recipe.
  • The “vision is solved” claim is a category error — it conflates generative image quality and captioning with physical understanding, and implicitly erases robotics.
  • No playbook exists. 10+ live approaches (imitation learning, RL, sim-to-real, offline RL, learning from video, VLAs, model-based RL, self-supervised exploration, diffusion policies) — because nobody has cracked zero-shot competence in an unseen situation.
The two camps on world models
  • Predictive (LeCun / JEPA): don't rely on seeing — predict embeddings or latent states. Max leans this way.
  • Generative: the best way to predict what's next is to generate the future as you move.
  • NVIDIA straddles both — it sells GPUs either way.
  • Working definition of a world model: intelligence that adapts without being trained for the situation.
Data is the bottleneck — and it dictates architecture
  • Most training techniques bottom out in collecting visual data — hence the capital flooding into training facilities and camera-headset recording rigs.
  • You physically can't stream video 24/7 to the cloud, so models must run on-device: tiny (~19MB, CPU-runnable), low-energy.
  • Edge models filter at the source — capturing true positives only. Data curation becomes part of the model, not a post-processing step.
  • Expect a data wall — and labs starting to share datasets as scarcity bites.
A training approach for small teams
  • Distillation is the on-ramp — the “Qwen playbook”: start from a large teacher (~72B) and distill down. Cheaper and faster than building from scratch.
  • Reasoning matters more than assumed: start from a reasoning model and teach it to see, rather than bolting reasoning onto a vision model.
  • Their VLA is a deliberate “Frankenstein”: a distilled reasoning model + a traditional VLM + a pure detection model, running ~10 bespoke primitives.
  • Their most advanced VLM was distilled from NVIDIA Cosmos (the reasoning model). Synthetic/sim data is still on the exploration list.
Real-world constraints researchers underrate
  • Low-res is fine. Benchmarks run at 640–1088px, so 15-year-old cameras still work at the pixel level. Don't let sensor quality gate data collection — start now.
  • The hard cases are the point: occlusion, rain, snow, garbage footage. Football was the proving ground — 22 agents in continuous chaos — and structure that survives that generalises to stores and stations.
  • Data access is a commercial problem, not a technical one. Enterprises won't hand over sensors until you solve a real problem first. The flywheel: deploy → gain camera access → keep updating weights from real operations.
  • Enterprise self-serve is economically impossible — on-site reasoning + vision needs A100s (H100s for real time) across tens of thousands of sites. That's the structural case for tiny on-device models.
The strategic read for researchers
  • Max is explicit that he's “upstream” — deliberately on the sidelines of the methodology race, collecting data and building models that reason over it while waiting to see which camp cracks it.
  • The takeaway: the data layer is the durable position while the learning paradigm is still unsettled.
