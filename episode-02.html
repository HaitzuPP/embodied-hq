Is robot learning a data problem, or an architecture problem?

NP MC With Nils Pihl ↗, Founder and CEO of Auki Labs, and Michael Cho ↗, Co-founder of BitRobot (FrodoBots Lab)

BitRobot's Michael Cho is building global infrastructure to collect in-the-wild robot data at scale. Auki's Nils Pihl thinks the data-collection gold rush is a mistake. Two credible operators with real deployments disagree on the central question, whether embodied intelligence is bottlenecked by data or by architecture, and the disagreement is the signal.

These are the written field notes from the conversation.

Two operators who have shipped real systems took opposite sides of the field's central question. The disagreement, not either answer, is the useful part.

Evaluation is the real bottleneck

Digital models iterate fast: run the benchmark, read the score, try again. Physical AI breaks that loop, because you cannot speed up reality. A million zero-disengagement miles of full self-driving still would not settle L4 versus L5 autonomy: the next mile might crash. Edge cases get rarer as you approach the tail, so confidence is harder to earn the closer you get. The field's rate limiter is evaluation infrastructure, not model capacity.

The brutal math of long-horizon reliability

Frying an egg decomposes into roughly a hundred sequential subtasks. Even at a generous ninety-nine percent on each, end-to-end success is 0.99 to the hundredth power, about thirty-seven percent. A robot that looks strong on any single step still fails two attempts in three across a real task. Usable means pushing each subtask toward four, five, even six nines, plus real recovery when a step fails.

Data problem, or architecture problem?

Here the guests split. Michael is building global infrastructure to collect in-the-wild robot data at scale, and bets data is the unlock. Nils calls the data-collection boom “one of the dumbest moments of VC groupthink ever”, and argues the bottleneck is architecture. Both are credible, both have real deployments, and the episode does not resolve it.

A radically more efficient path may exist

A mosquito navigates the physical world on roughly a tenth of a watt, with almost no pretraining and extraordinary data efficiency. Biology is proof that a fundamentally more efficient approach to physical intelligence exists, a real argument that scaling, the strategy that worked for language, is not the answer here. Michael puts robotics at its GPT-2 moment: new versus a year ago, but far from the wake-up.

How the market actually works

BitRobot runs as a two-sided market: a global fleet of cheap teleoperated robots supplies the data, labs consume it, and real-world evaluation on distributed robots is the benchmark. The moat is not the model. Data access is a commercial and relationship problem, not a technical one, a theme across all three episodes.
