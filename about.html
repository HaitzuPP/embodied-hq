About

A serious show about how machines learn to act in the physical world.

Robot learning is at an inflection point, moving from papers to working prototypes, and the field has no settled playbook. Embodied HQ goes deep with the people building it.

What it is

A podcast on robot learning, made for people who work in it

Embodied HQ is a podcast about generalist robots and the research that will decide whether they work. The most interesting conversations in the field happen in private, between the researchers and operators actually building this. We put them on the record, in depth, for an audience that already knows the terrain.

It is written for academic and lab researchers, and for the technically serious people around them. We assume you know what a VLA is. We do not simplify to reach a wider crowd.

How each episode is made

Long-form conversations, turned into durable field notes

Each episode is a long conversation with a researcher or operator on the front lines. We then turn every conversation into a structured set of notes: the arguments, the open problems, and the claims worth keeping. Those notes live on the Research page, so the ideas stay useful long after the audio.

What we cover

The questions the show chases

  • Perception. Why computer vision still has no consensus training recipe, and why that gap is the whole game.
  • World models. The two camps, predictive versus generative, and what a world model actually is.
  • Data. Why the scarcity of real-world video dictates the architecture, and pushes models onto the edge.
  • Evaluation and reliability. Why you can't speed up reality, and why long-horizon reliability is the real bottleneck.
  • The messy real world. Occlusion, chaos, privacy, and the environments robots have to survive.
Editorial stance

Technical, and not credulous

We treat a demo as an upper bound, not a capability. We care about results that hold up over long, uncut runs, not one-minute highlight reels. We are happy to leave a question unresolved when the honest answer is that the field has not settled it, because a productive disagreement between two credible operators is often more useful than a tidy conclusion.

No hype. No vendor talking points.
