Is robot learning a data problem, or an architecture problem?

NP MC With Nils Pihl ↗, Founder and CEO of Auki Labs, and Michael Cho ↗, Co-founder of BitRobot (FrodoBots Lab)

BitRobot's Michael Cho is building global infrastructure to collect in-the-wild robot data at scale. Auki's Nils Pihl thinks the data-collection gold rush is a mistake. Two credible operators with real deployments disagree on the central question, whether embodied intelligence is bottlenecked by data or by architecture, and the disagreement is the signal.

Evaluation is the real bottleneck. You can't speed up reality.
Digital models iterate fast because benchmarks are near-instant. Physical AI is the opposite: a million zero-disengagement FSD miles still wouldn't prove L4 vs L5, because the next mile might crash. Edge cases get rarer at the tail, so confidence gets harder to earn the closer you get.
The subtask math is brutal: 0.99¹⁰⁰ ≈ 37%.
Frying an egg is roughly 100 sequential subtasks. Even at a generous 99% each, end-to-end success collapses to about 37%. The frontier is long-horizon reliability and recovery, roughly six nines per subtask, not marginal per-task gains.
Mosquito intelligence: 0.1W, no pretraining.
A mosquito navigates the physical world data-efficiently on a fraction of a watt, never pretrained on millions of hours. Biology is proof a radically more efficient approach exists, a real argument that scaling is not the answer here.

Full episode notes
The two strongest technical insights
  • Evaluation is the rate limiter, and it can't be compressed. Digital models iterate fast because benchmark results are near-instant. Physical AI is the opposite. Even a million zero-disengagement FSD miles wouldn't tell you L4 vs L5, because the next mile might crash. Edge cases get rarer as you approach the tail, so confidence gets harder the closer you are. The bottleneck is eval infrastructure, not model capacity.
  • The subtask compounding math is a usability problem, not a safety one. SOTA short-horizon success sits around 70 to 80%. Frying an egg is roughly 100 sequential subtasks. Even at 99% each, 0.99^100 ≈ 37% end-to-end. You need roughly six nines per subtask plus strong recovery. Long-horizon reliability and error recovery deserve far more attention than marginal per-task gains.
Where the field's assumptions may be wrong
  • “Mosquito intelligence.” Roughly 0.1W, extraordinarily data-efficient, never pretrained on millions of hours of flight. Biology proves a fundamentally different, radically more efficient approach to physical intelligence exists, a real argument that scaling is not the answer.
  • Language pretraining doesn't transfer to perception and movement. The entire internet text corpus isn't a meaningful leap toward teaching a robot to see and move. VLAs translate intent (“unscrew the bottle cap”) but that doesn't mean the model knows what the hands must do. That data doesn't exist yet.
  • A contrarian shot at the data-collection boom. Nils is openly bearish on VC-funded robot data collection, predicting we'll look back on it as “one of the dumbest moments of VC groupthink ever.” If the mosquito point holds, the bottleneck is architecture, not more teleop hours.
  • Embodied models are compressions of their datasets. Train on a handful of Airbnb kitchens and you overfit to them. Every kitchen is similar and every kitchen is unique. That is the honest case for in-the-wild data, in direct tension with the point above. The episode doesn't resolve it, which is itself the signal.
Epistemic hygiene for reading the field
  • Treat a demo as an upper bound, not a capability. Assume the one-minute video is the ceiling and may have taken hours of takes. Uncut, hours-long runs are the only real proof. Many impressive humanoid demos are still substantially teleoperated.
  • Where we actually are: Michael puts robotics at roughly the GPT-2 moment. Something qualitatively new versus a year ago, but a large gap before the GPT-3-style wake-up. His timeline is digital AGI within a few years, robotics still unsolved. Physical AI is where the remaining hard research lives.
Practical resources researchers can use
  • BitRobot's explicit model is to do the work “researchers wanted done but shouldn't have spent time doing.” That means data collection, compute aggregation, and real-world evaluation of models on physical robots distributed globally.
  • Concrete proof: a UC Berkeley lab built a SOTA urban-navigation model on the FrodoBots dataset and evaluated it on robots in Africa and South America, geographic diversity no single lab can obtain. Human-versus-AI competitions drew people from Google DeepMind, Berkeley, NUS, and UT Austin. They are releasing what they claim is the largest urban-navigation dataset of its kind on Hugging Face.
  • Data IP default: open-source for non-commercial use, with commercial licensing split between the subnet owner and the foundation. Worth knowing if you're evaluating data provenance.
  • The cheap-hardware insight: a $10,000 robot is a bad data-collection device. They built a roughly $500 teleoperated “dumb” robot and made data collection a byproduct of a game people wanted to play.
Two constraints researchers systematically underweight
  • Privacy will shape what data is legally and socially available. Both 1X and Tesla have said openly they intend to build the largest training set in the world by recording inside people's homes. Expect friction. That is an argument for on-device, federated, and self-hosted architectures over centralized hoarding. Auki deliberately collects no data.
  • The environment is a design variable, not a fixed constraint. Cities were reshaped for cars. They will be reshaped for robots. If SLAM and visual positioning fail on repeating patterns, buildings will adopt non-repeating “visual entropy” surfaces, and minimalism and repeating tiles disappear. Expect battery-swap stations, repair shops, and drone pads too. You don't always have to solve perception in a hostile environment when co-designing the environment is on the table, which also punctures the “humanoids are perfectly suited to our world” story.
The open question
  • The two guests disagree productively. Michael thinks in-the-wild data at massive scale is the unlock and is building infrastructure for it. Nils thinks the data-collection gold rush is a mistake. Both are credible operators with real deployments.
  • Is embodied intelligence a data problem or an architecture problem? That unresolved tension is arguably the open question in robot learning right now.
