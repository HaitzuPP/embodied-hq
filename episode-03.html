Is robotics repeating the AR/VR hype cycle?

LS With Lex Sokolin ↗, Managing Partner and Co-Founder of Generative Ventures

Lex invests at Generative Ventures, at the intersection of fintech, AI, and crypto, a thesis he calls the machine economy. He argues robotics is a retread of the LLM race with far more friction, warns that the hype will outrun physical reality the way AR/VR did in 2018, and makes the case that durable value sits in infrastructure, data access, and the system around the model, not the model itself.

The audio launches with the show. For now these are the written field notes, free to read.

Key takeaways
Robotics today looks like AR/VR in 2018.
Back then AR/VR had the same shape: a hardware-shipment story, big players, and an “every iPhone becomes a headset” narrative. It tallied out at roughly 10 million headsets a year and became, in his words, a nothing burger. Today only 5,000 to 7,000 Unitree robots shipped, while Figure carries a roughly $40B valuation with no real revenue.
Being smart and doing the right trade are separate things.
You can be correct that robotics is too early and still be wrong about where the capital goes. Funding will be abundant even if the technology isn't ready, and a winter is possible on the other side of that.
There is no defensibility in the best model.
Open source catches up and compresses it fast. Defensibility has moved to the harness, the integrations, the data, evaluation, and deployment. The model is a depreciating asset; the durable position is the system around it.
Data access is a moat, and it is commercial, not technical.
Data companies bootstrapping datasets to sell to labs are undifferentiated unless they have privileged access. The one Lex backed cleared the bar only because it had access to two factories. That is a third independent confirmation of a theme running through all three episodes.

Full episode notes
The AR/VR cautionary analogy
  • Lex's sharpest point. In 2018, AR/VR had exactly today's shape: a hardware-shipment story, big players (Google, Apple, Magic Leap), and an “every iPhone becomes a headset” narrative. It tallied out at roughly 10 million headsets a year and became, in his words, a nothing burger.
  • The sobering comparison. Around 5,000 Unitree robots shipped last year, maybe 7,000 globally. Narrative volume vastly exceeds deployment volume. Figure carries a roughly $40B valuation with no real revenue to speak of.
  • The caveat is the important half. Being smart and doing the right trade are separate things. You can be correct that robotics is too early and still be wrong about where the capital goes. Funding will be abundant even if the technology isn't ready, and a winter is possible on the other side of that.
Where research value accrues
  • There is no long-term defensibility in having the best model. Open source catches up and compresses it fast. Defensibility has moved to the harness, the integrations, and the connectivity into clients. The model is a depreciating asset; the durable position is the system around it, meaning data, evaluation, and deployment. Max reached the same conclusion from the technical side, which makes it worth taking seriously.
  • Infrastructure paid off, applications did not. In the LLM boom, investing in memory, GPUs, and compute paid off; investing in companies selling AI products didn't. The same shape is forming in robotics, with value concentrating in infrastructure and manufacturing. The infrastructure and data layers have been the reliable place to stand.
  • A retread with more friction. Robotics is a retread of the LLM race, but it cannot move at software speed. The hype will outrun the physical reality, which is the exact mechanism that produced the AR/VR outcome.
  • Data access is a moat, not a technique. Data companies bootstrapping datasets to sell to labs are undifferentiated unless they have privileged access. One Lex backed only cleared the bar because it had access to two factories. Data access is a relationship and commercial moat, not a technical one, a third independent confirmation of the theme running through all three episodes.
Two genuine research openings
  • Non-humanoid morphologies are underexplored whitespace. His portfolio company Imitation Labs is built on this: everyone is using world models to teach humanoids to walk like people, but the same learning methods apply to swimming robots, excavators, and forms that aren't shaped like us. Less competition, clearer commercial pull, and it quietly punctures the humanoid-centric assumption Nils also attacked.
  • Neuro-AI as a reference class for efficient physical intelligence. Neto Labs, co-invested with Protocol Labs, implants tiny neural interfaces in mice, houses them in heavily instrumented environments, and correlates neural activity with how the animal constructs motion and figures out its surroundings. This connects directly to Michael Cho's mosquito-intelligence argument: biology solves physical intelligence on roughly 0.1 W with almost no pretraining, and a fundamental architectural breakthrough is still missing. If that is the real bottleneck, this line of work is closer to the answer than another million teleop hours.
Context worth knowing about the funders
  • The capital wave comes from big-tech balance sheets, $100B to $300B in cash redirected from buybacks into the AI race. Lab valuations are being set with what Lex compares to 2022 Binance layer-one levels of rigor, for example $500M at a $2B pre on a seed. If you are in a well-funded lab, some of that funding is narrative, not fundamentals.
  • The age of the genius. Robotics and AI founders are academics and scientists, not managers. Lex expects this to produce meaningful capital destruction, and expects industrialists like Bezos and Musk to move in and use the scientists. Worth internalizing if you are a researcher who wants to own the commercialization of your own work rather than have it captured.
  • China plus physical intelligence is rerating Asia. China's manufacturing capacity, combined with the physical-intelligence race, is driving a rerating of Asian equities. That is where the deployment volume will concentrate.
