Design system v1

A restrained, technical system. Black canvas, machine vision, and almost no ornament.

Embodied HQ is a podcast about bringing generalist robots into the real world. The design system is built to feel like the instrument panel of a perception system: precise, calm, and confident, with exactly one moment of colour per view. This document is the source of truth. Everything here is live on embodiedhq.io.

01

Principles

If a decision isn't covered below, these six rules decide it.

Restraint is the brand

Two font weights, one accent colour, no bold, no shadows, no gradients. Confidence is communicated by how little we use, not how much.

Whitespace does the structuring

Content blocks are flat and borderless. Hairline rules and generous vertical space create hierarchy. We do not use cards, rounded containers, or drop shadows for content.

One highlight per view

Yellow is reserved for a single focal moment (the rotating hero word, a funding figure, a launch badge). If yellow appears twice on a screen, one of them is wrong.

Black is where the data lives

Light surfaces carry reading. Black, full-bleed surfaces carry the machine-vision imagery and the proof (funding, data, signal). The contrast between the two is the core rhythm of the brand.

Motion is slow and deliberate

Every transition is 0.6s ease-in-out. Nothing snaps. The system should feel like a sensor sweeping, not an app responding.

Left-aligned, always

Headings, body, and eyebrows all align left. Centred text is reserved for standalone graphics and covers.

06

The signature motif: machine vision

This is the most important asset in the brand and the thing that makes us recognisable. It is a live perception system rendered as art: a black field, animated 3D wireframe detection boxes drifting and rotating in perspective, corner-bracket trackers, confidence labels, and a scan line sweeping the frame and locking onto objects as it passes.

Rules for using it

Always on pure black. Always in the four signal colours. Objects are always labelled with a plausible class and a confidence score (ROBOT ARM · 0.98). Never place it behind long-form reading copy without a gradient veil to protect legibility. It should feel calm, not frantic: slow drift, slow rotation, one scan pass every few seconds.

Where it appears

The site hero, the social share image, the Substack cover, episode thumbnails, and any key art. It is the visual shorthand for "a machine perceiving the world," which is the entire subject of the show. Designers should extend this system rather than introduce a new one.

09

Voice (for anyone writing UI copy)

Grounded, clear, excited. First person, plain speech. We translate, we don't dumb down. No hype, no doom.

We sound like

  • "Conversations with the researchers and operators on the front lines."
  • "The money has already decided this matters"
  • "Explained plainly, no PhD required."
  • "Free. No spam, unsubscribe anytime."

We never sound like

  • "Revolutionary breakthrough unleashes the future of AI."
  • Punchy sentence fragments stacked for drama.
  • Unexplained jargon used to signal intelligence.
  • Em dashes. We don't use them, anywhere.
10

Do & don't

Do

  • Use hairlines and whitespace to create hierarchy.
  • Keep every transition at 0.6s ease-in-out.
  • Reserve yellow for exactly one focal moment per view.
  • Put data and imagery on black, reading on white.
  • Extend the machine-vision motif for new key art.
  • Label detected objects with a class and a confidence score.

Don't

  • Add bold weight, ever. 400 and 500 only.
  • Use gradients, drop shadows, or glow effects.
  • Put content in rounded cards with borders.
  • Introduce a second accent colour or a new palette.
  • Centre body copy or headings.
  • Use stock robot photography. It kills the brand instantly.
