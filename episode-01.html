Why vision is still waiting for its GPT moment

MS With Max Sebti ↗, CEO of Manako Labs

Manako builds vision agents that run entirely on-device, in 19MB models that turn camera networks into real-time understanding. Max argues computer vision still has no consensus training recipe, explains why that gap defines the field, and makes the case that the data layer, not the learning paradigm, is the durable place to stand.

These are the written field notes from the conversation.

Manako runs vision agents entirely on-device, in models small enough to fit on a CPU. The throughline: computer vision still has no consensus recipe, and that absence shapes everything downstream.

The data problem forces the architecture

Start with a constraint that sounds like plumbing but is strategy. You cannot stream every camera's video to the cloud around the clock: there are not enough data centers on Earth. So the model runs where the camera is, tiny and on-device, curating true positives at the source. Data does not just train the model, it dictates the architecture. Manako's detection models are about 19MB and run on a CPU because the physics of data leave no other option.

Vision is still waiting for its GPT moment

There is no settled architecture because vision never got language's breakthrough. Transformers gave language one recipe everyone could build on. Vision has roughly ten live approaches and no consensus, from imitation learning to diffusion policies, because none has cracked zero-shot competence in a situation the robot has never seen. The claim that vision is solved is a category error: generative image quality is not physical understanding, and saying vision is solved quietly erases robotics, because robotics runs on vision.

The two camps on world models

Underneath the method zoo sits one real disagreement: whether you need to see at all. The predictive camp, associated with JEPA, predicts latent states rather than pixels. The generative camp renders the future frame by frame. Max's definition cuts through it: a world model is intelligence that adapts without being trained for the situation in front of it. NVIDIA straddles both, since it sells the GPUs either way.

The enterprise flywheel

None of this reaches the world without data, and data access is a commercial problem, not a technical one. Manako's loop is concrete: deploy on-device, solve a real problem, earn camera access, then update the weights from real operations. Access is won in the field, and then it compounds.

What a small team actually ships

The result is a deliberate Frankenstein: a large teacher distilled into a reasoning model, stitched to a traditional VLM and a pure detection model, running about ten bespoke primitives. Reasoning first, sight second. The durable work is the system around the model, not any single model.
